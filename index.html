Vine Encomium

Six Seconds of Fame: A Vine Encomium

Vine, released in 2013 under Twitter’s ownership, immediately skyrocketed in popularity, captivating a global audience like no social media had done before. Vine wasn’t just any social media app, it took the world by storm because of its unique but simple form of content. For the first time, an app completely focused on short form looping videos, giving anyone the chance to go viral for anything, including comedy, music, acting, and more. However, this new, creative idea would soon be taken over by newer competitors like Snapchat and TikTok, and Vine's steady downfall would begin. In 2016, Twitter, who had acquired the app in 2012, announced that the platform would be shut down and removed from the app store in the coming months. This shocking news launched an outpour of tributes, not only from its popular creators, but also an entire generation who grew up on the app. While Vine's shutdown marked the end and start of a new era of social media, its influence on pop culture and the social media environment persists even 8 years later.

With Vine’s innovative six-second format not only introducing a new way to consume content but also creating stars who remain household names today, its huge impact on pop culture can’t be denied (Tran, 2023). These creators included musicians like Shawn Mendes, comedians like King Bach, and video editors like Zach King, proving that any kind of content had potential to gain traction on the app. These viral videos transcended Vine, spreading across other platforms, reaching those who never even had the app, and becoming part of the broader internet culture (Rios, 2021). Iconic Vine quotes and clips such as, “what are those?" or a potato flying around a room, will forever exist in the social media vocabulary and continue to be referenced.

Despite Vine’s short-lived popularity and abrupt shutdown in 2017, the app has had a lasting impact on the current social media environment by paving the way for newer platforms. Vine was the first social media platform to include auto playing short-form videos, allowing for the rise of new types of content creators, audiences, and ways to go viral. Popular Vine creators were some of the first influencers that we now have a surplus of on other platforms like Instagram. As mentioned earlier, stars like Logan Paul and Shawn Mendes got their start after their 6 second videos blew up. Vine was the first example we had of a new genre of content creators, then called Viners, who could go viral and build a fanbase off only a few seconds of content (Dew, 2024). The app even became a helpful tool for the music industry, with both new artists, like Ruth B, and existing artists, like Drake, benefitting from the platform's community and new meme culture (Spanos, 2016).

An article published on Vox shortly after the shutdown was announced states that the most common comment from non-users was “I just don’t get Vine,” something that most people couldn’t fathom hearing now. Every social media focuses on short-form videos to some degree now, so imagining social media before this creation is a difficult task. The article also mentions that 6 seconds is the ideal amount of time for both the creator and the consumer, because “you can accomplish a lot in six seconds” (Romano, 2016). With a better way to make and interact with content, Vine became a space where people from every corner of the internet could come together and be part of the same community, “providing a perfect, innocuous interruption and respite from the routine of our daily lives,” (Wong, 2022).

This idea would soon be followed up by Instagram, Snapchat, YouTube, and most importantly, TikTok (formerly Musical.ly). TikTok launched in 2014 under the name Musical.ly, with a very similar concept to Vine, where creators could post 15 second videos with either music playing over or using an original sound. This new app gained traction slowly but blew up in 2017 -as Vine’s reign came to an end- after being acquired by ByteDance and merging with TikTok (Jalan, 2022). Many Vine creators made the switch to TikTok, lots of them switching even before the shutdown, and were able to continue making the same kind of content on a more versatile and opportune platform (Jalan, 2022). Without Vine, we wouldn’t have the apps and creators that we now love so much.

In the four years that we had Vine, it had one of the biggest impacts on how we currently interact and consume media, some may argue even more than platforms like Facebook and Tumblr that are still around. Vine transformed social media by making short form videos the norm, a trend still prominent on other apps, inventing an easier way to create and to consume content, proving how even in a short amount of time, an extraordinary change can be made.

Why I chose Vine

I chose to use Vine for my encomium because of my personal experience with the app. I was only 7 when the app came out, but it was my first experience with social media. As the app blew up, I started seeing them on YouTube and hearing my older brothers talking about them. The main contributing factor however, was my older cousin. She was moderately popular on the app, launching her career as a comedian, and she featured me in a few of her Vines. I vividly remember visiting her family in Connecticut and helping her make a video in the backyard. This initial exposure to the app had me hooked, and while I was too young to have a phone at the time, I was able to download the app on my moms iPad. I still remember watching the first Vine that showed up when I downloaded the app, a couple singing the song "Santeria" by Sublime. This discovery launched me into the world of social media and not soon after I found Musical.ly (which would become TikTok). When I found out that the app was shutting down, I felt the way that most other watchers did, and always hoped it would come back. Writing my encomium about Vine was my way of brining it back into my life.
